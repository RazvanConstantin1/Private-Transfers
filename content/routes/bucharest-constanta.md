## About Constanța and the Black Sea Coast

Constanța has 2,600 years of recorded history, making it one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in Romania. The old town occupies a small peninsula jutting into the Black Sea, layered with Greek, Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman, and Romanian history.

**In the city:**

- **Ovid Square and Ovid Statue** — The Roman poet Ovid spent his exile in Tomis (Constanța) from 8 AD until his death in 17 AD. The central square commemorates him.
- **National History and Archaeology Museum** — Houses the extraordinary Glykon serpent statue (2nd century AD) and an extensive Roman collection.
- **The Roman Mosaic** — A 4th-century Roman mosaic floor covering 2,000 square metres, preserved beneath a protective building in the old port area.
- **Carol I Mosque** — Built in 1910 by King Carol I, the mosque's 50-metre minaret is the tallest in Romania and a Constanța landmark.
- **The Casino** — A stunning Belle Époque building (1910) on the seafront, currently under long-running restoration; striking regardless.

**The coast:**

- **Mamaia** — Romania's most popular beach resort, 8 km north of Constanța. A narrow strip of land between the Black Sea and Siutghiol Lake, lined with hotels, clubs, and beach bars. Best for lively summer atmosphere.
- **Vama Veche** — 60 km south, near the Bulgarian border. The most bohemian, alternative beach village in Romania — small, unpretentious, festival-friendly.
- **Neptun and Olimp** — Quieter family resorts 35 km south, with good value accommodation and calmer beaches.
- **Histria** — 60 km north of Constanța, the ruins of the oldest Greek colony in Romania (7th century BC), on a lagoon near the Danube Delta.
